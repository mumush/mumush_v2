Context AwarenessJuly 27, 2015

I've been doing a lot of ideation lately, and one of the biggest findings was that the Watch can be context aware of what is happening in the mobile companion app. The Watch becomes extremely useful as a context aware reference point for information that would be too bothersome to take your phone out and get yourself. Instead, the Watch surfaces it for you.

Think about a scenario in which you're looking at your phone to check the bus schedule. You then lock your phone, and continue to walk towards the bus stop. As you raise your wrist to glance at the time, you press the Digital Crown, which then takes you not to the home screen, but instead immediately to the companion bus watch app. It shows you only the most important information — the time the bus you were just looking at in the mobile app is going to arrive, and whether or not it's early or late.

We can extend the bus example further to touch on the benefits of time-sensitive notifications: I usually start to walk to the bus stop around 6:30, in order to catch the bus by 7:00, but its arrival time fluctuates. So how do I know if the bus is early, on-time, or late? Well, I open the app to find out. But what if the app merely sent you a notification saying "Hey dude, hurry up! Your bus is early and will be here in 9 minutes." The beauty here is that you as the user didn't have to go out and get that crucial information, it instead came to you through a tap on the wrist, and it could be the difference between you waiting another hour for the next bus in a sour mood.

It seems like this context, or flow aware, use case of the Watch can have huge implications on how we access data, and even more so, how that data can come to us on the raise of our wrist.

Leveraging Z-SpaceMay 4, 2015

Our devices are two-dimensional, but the interface displayed on these screens doesn't have to be. Almost every interface designed for mobile and web apps uses two dimesions, in which the pieces of the interface appear to be neatly layed on top of the screen. Why?

On the desktop, OS X in particular, depth is used to effectively present a layered interface in which different windows of content can be hierarchically presented above one another, which decreases the gap between real world interactions, and those done through a screen — it makes the interface feel more realistic because it's more than just a picture frame.

If you've ever launched Time Machine on OS X, you'll remember the three-dimensional timeline that you can navigate through to access a backup from way back when. The first time I saw that, I was absolutely blown away, and frankly kind of scared (which is something to be said about these sorts of interfaces), by the way that I was able to actually move through the interface rather than simply act on it.

Interfaces that effectively use depth make an in-app experience on a phone or web browser feel more like an entrance into a new "space," rather than a new picture in the same frame. The former creates a sense of immersion that all mobile and web applications seek, which inevitably create a beautifully addicting application.

MotivateMarch 8, 2015

School work has kept me super busy lately, especially with my Human Centered Requirements & Design class. I've been heavily invested in our semester long project that requires teams to build one highly usable application on any platform. I'm really excited about where our app stands right now, despite the fact that we've started coding, or high fidelity design yet.

Our app, that we just recently renamed to "Motivate," was originally slated to make it easy to log food/nutrition information for those who are trying to lose/gain weight, or those who are recovering/recovered from an eating disorder. Our idea was relatively simple, and had been done before.

This past weekend, we realized that many existing diet tracking apps fail to do one massively important thing when it comes to dieting effectively: motivate their users to continue toward their goal weight. Our project aims to capitalize on this through the use of visual comparitive motivation.

After a lot of research, we learned that the best way to motivate someone who for example, is trying to get thinner, is through comparitive imagery. This however does not apply for those who have had, or are recovering from an eating disorder. Below I'll list some of the ideas we generated, feasible or not.

In the "dashboard" view of our app, where you see how close you are to your dietary goals for the day, there is always a different image in the header that features someone who you physically aspire to look like. Mixing all of these interesting motivational approaches could prove to be extremely effective in helping users stay dedicated to their diet. Based on our interviews, we found that users often lose motivation to continue with their diet after some time because they don't see any visual changes quick enough. We feel the motivational concepts mentioned above could help, and I'm interested to see how effective the app really is.

Weather Glyph UpdateFebruary 25, 2015

I'm making pretty good progress with the first round of weather glyphs! Forecast gives you an "icon" dictionary that could have one of 12 different values like "partly-cloudy-day" or "sleet" which is super convenient, so I've been working on glyphs for each of those possible states.

I was on a serious roll but at some point got stuck on a layering issue that I didn't entirely understand — I guess that's sort of expected considering I don't have a ton of experience creating glyphs.

I wanted to have the outline of part of the sun shown behind the outline of a cloud, but every time I tried, I ended up with a cloud showing the bottom half of the sun...ugh. The solution was a mixture of a few things. I first had to take the existing cloud and sun, and align them just right (this would result in the bottom of the sun being visible inside the cloud). I then made sure the cloud layer was on top of the sun (ensuring the subtraction would work correctly), and then duplicated the cloud layer. I then subtracted the cloud layer from the sun layer, yielding a piece of the sun, and the remaining portion of the duplicated cloud in front of it.

I'm not quite sure if this is the correct approach, but it did the trick for me!

A Chrome ExtensionFebruary 3, 2015

Just as I was starting to get into the swing of things back at school, I stumbled upon a problem in Chrome that I hadn't yet solved. Every time I think I'll "read a page later," I pin it to the tab bar in Chrome. More often than not, I'll wind up with 10+ pinned tabs, none of which I can see the title for. A few days ago I saw a guy who legitimately had half of the tab bar full of pinned tabs, so I knew it wasn't alone.

A bit after this realization, I noticed how I use Chrome's "New Tab" page to click some websites I don't have pinned tabs for, but visit often. I've seen quite a few Chrome Extensions that do a lot with the page, and look beautiful — but they take slightly too long to load. It really is just another HTML file. Given the fact that most users create a new tab to then type in a url or do a direct search on Google, the time spent on the New Tab page is often quite small. Because of this, waiting for content to load is not an option.

I decided to make a Chrome Extension that allows you to "pin a tab" to the New Tab page. It's achieved through the use of a browser action button. Upon clicking the button on a tab that you would normally just pin to read later, nothing changes on your tab bar. Instead, a screenshot, the page title, and the url are taken from the tab and are saved to chrome.storage behind the scenes. Now, when you open a new tab you'll see something like this:

You'll notice that the pages you would have normally just pinned to read later, now appear on the new tab page similar to Chrome's existing new tab page. Upon clicking one of the screenshot images, you're taken to the page you were planning on reading later. As you would assume, when you hover over one of the images, an "x" appears just like the default new tab page in Chrome. This was all on purpose. The goal was to make this extension feel like it was built into Chrome by default.

In the top right of Chrome you'll notice this extensions' browser icon to the right of the address bar. Due to this screenshots quality, it's tough to see that the icon presents a "pin" inside of a tab that is pinned to the tab bar. Again, the goal here was to make everything look like it was a part of Chrome by default, so that is where the icon inspiration came from.

The name of the extension is relatively straight forward, which is actually quite the opposite of the way I usually name my projects: Read Tab Later. You can find the extension here in the Chrome Web Store. I'm hoping this extension proves to be useful for others who find their tab bars equally as cluttered as mine used to be!

A Future Without AppsJanuary 14, 2015

Recently I've been doing a lot of thinking about apps, and their role in future systems like mobile phones. I had this thought that there may not even be a need for stand alone apps, so I figured I'd try to expand on it.

On a day that involves some waiting, I'll pull out my phone and check Pinterest. After I've scrolled down long enough, I move on to Medium and look for some interesting tech/design articles. I don't have the Facebook or Twitter apps on my phone, but we can assume that a large number of users go to these apps at some point during their waiting period. My dad uses a few different news apps as well as a third-party weather app. Let's not forget Instagram, Yik Yak, Tinder, Vine...the list could go on and on depending on the user. In the majority of these apps, users are consuming content to pass time.

What if our mobile devices didn't need to install a standalone app, and only needed to make a behind the scenes request to a [web] service in order to gather content? What if that connection was made as soon as you woke up your device? Once that connection was made, your device would then display content that was most relevant to what you wanted, or needed without opening anything — it was just there.

This idea was generated from an iOS app I used called Ping, which has the goal of giving you the content you want so you don't need to search for it. Think about that statement. That's what we should be pushing for. How can we give our users the content they want before they hear about it? Ping does this by sending you a push notification, which isn't the ideal experience if you're familiar with the way push notifications work from a programmatic standpoint, but it gets pretty damn close to providing content without needing to open an app at all.

A few months ago Github announced its Student Developer Pack, which made relatively expensive services available to students through a number of huge discounts. This was a huge deal. Some of the discounts were crazy — I don't have to pay for my web hosting for a year. That crazy. As soon as this was announced, I got a push notification from Ping giving me the low down. I mean how sweet is that? I had to do absolutely nothing, and got a massive reward for it.

I think the future of content consumption and the resulting interfaces lie in the mechanics of this app, and it's something I'd love to work on.

On the Word “Fine”December 10, 2014

One word I absolutely cannot stand is the word "fine." Examples: I'm fine, It's fine, I did fine. None of these sound genuinely positive, and every time I hear these phrases, I have to cringe.

Life is not worth being "fine." The word screams unsatisfied, and more often than not, unhappy. I constantly tell my parents to stop being "fine" with things because they don't have any more time to waste. When they tell me that they're fine with how things are going when they're doing something they don't want to do, I can't help but be upset.

I have no interest in being fine, doing fine, or doing things that are fine, and you shouldn't either. Doing fine work isn't doing good, great, excellent, or exceptional work; and that should be enough to make you want to do more than fine.

Doing fine work is what gets you a C in a class that you're clearly capable of getting an A in. It's staying off of the radar. You, and your work do not do anything risky, groundbreaking, or compelling. This is because doing fine work doesn't require much thought at all. Fine work requires the minimum amount of effort to get by.

Being fine is not taking control of your current situation, whether that be a relationship, job, friendship, or whatever else you can think of being fine with. "Yea whatever, I'm cool with it, it's nothing huge but it's getting me by." If you don't like something, change it. If I find myself saying something is fine, I know I have to re-evaluate why I'm doing it.

I'll never forget the end of my internship last summer when I asked my mentor: "Did I do great, or did I do fine here?" I asked my mentor to choose one. If he said that I did fine, I knew I had failed myself. I could not have felt happier to hear him say that I did great.

Whenever I do things, I look at my work and ask myself if what I did is great, or if it's fine. If in my eyes what I did is great, then I know that I have put everything I have into the project, and there's nothing better than realizing that.

The people who do great things, are never the ones that are okay with being fine.

Predictive Text SpacingNovember 17, 2014

The "Predictive" keyboard feature introduced in iOS 8 presents an interesting barrier if you're making an iOS app that depends on the vertical space between the top of the keyboard and another UI element. What's more, I can't find an API method to disable/hide the feature for the keyboard in a textfield you're using in your app. The only solution I've seen, is to completely disable auto correct...Sure, it makes your life easier as a developer, but it will make the lives of your users far worse if they have to keep backspacing for every word entered. You take that sort of thing for granted after using it for so long. I remember when the popover suggestions first came out as a feature, and I tried to turn off auto correct because I thought the popover suggestions were annoying...It was more annoying realizing how bad I was at typing without auto correct.

On my iPhone 5S I have the Predictive feature turned off, however on the Simulator, the feature is enabled. This reminded me that I need to account for spacing between the top of the keyboard and the scroll view above it, whether the predictive feature was enabled or not. In addition, I'd need to provide appropriate spacing in case the suggestion drawer was visible, or hidden (by pulling the drawer down).

When testing on the 5s, things looked good - except when I tried turning on the predictive feature. If I did this, a bit of the scrollView, positioned above the keyboard, would be covered (suggestion drawer open or not). Not ideal. Additionally, on the 4s, all of the scrollView was covered, with or without the predictive text. When it came to any other devices, they were tall enough to not present an issue.

The solution for this spacing issue was to do a nice constraint animation on the scrollView and all views above it, when the keyboard was about to come into view. By matching the animation rate of the keyboard sliding up with the rate used to animate all the above views, you get an illusion that the keyboard is pushing the content up along with it. This looked really nice and provided space for the predictive feature, enabled or not, drawer open or not. When it came to the 4s, simply hiding any views that the keyboard overlayed as it slid up was enough. Sliding the views above the keyboard up would result in the views being chopped off by the top of the screen, and a removal of context from the sentence (Is _ingredient_ Dairy Free?).

App submission is next Monday - I've marked it as such. I'm super excited to see how the app can help people with food allergies, like me.

I said I would stick with it, but I knew there was something off about my design. I knew I was doing it, but I really thought the Natural Language Form was sharp and wanted a cool way to put that technique into practice. A made an excuse to put the design to work. This is something I haven't faced before, as I normally start with a functional idea, and then generate the design, but the creation of this app was the opposite, which is kind of cool. It's crazy that when you take things away in an interface, it more often than not makes it better.

A Cool Design ProblemOctober 23, 2014

I'm using a natural language form as the only view in my app. So imagine the only objects on the screen being the words: Is _ingredient_ dairy free. The only piece of text the user can interact with is the _ingredient_ textfield. So apply a few different ingredients to the above text. Is corn dairy free? Is spinach gluten free? Is black beans soy free? Wait - what? When the ingredient entered is plural, the natural language is no longer natural. With ingredients, sometimes plurality is necessary without making the ingredient sound extremely unnatural. So how should I deal with this?

My first thought was: As soon as they hit the return key to hide the keyboard, and before they hit the search button, run a query to see if the food they entered is plural and then change the "Is" to "Are"...This is surely unnecessary overhead, and won't really make the experience any better. The reason is because changing the "Is" to "Are" after the user has entered the ingredient is meaningless. They've already thought about how to make the word not plural...aka they've already thought too much. It would just be a weird experience.

My current thought is to do the following. Make the "Is" and the "Gluten Free?" labels swipe-able. Imagine moving through photos in your Photos app on your phone. Swipe left to move to a new photo, right to an old one. I can apply the same concept here, but instead of photos, I'll make the labels swipe-able. This gives the user this cool sense of power to form any sentence they want easily, but more importantly, it makes sense to them. The cool part about this is that the choice from the "Is" or "Are" label is actually irrelevant. It has no influence on the result of the query; it's purely there for the users benefit.

I thought about this last night and haven't drawn anything out yet, but I can see a potential issue when it comes to affording the interaction. I need to come up with the best way to let the user know: "Hey, you see those labels above and below where you type the ingredient in? Yea, swipe right to get different ones." I actually should rephrase the prior statement. I need to come up with the most unobtrusive way to show users that this functionality exists in order to keep my design as minimal as possible. This is going to be a fun one.
